1. Concept Overview

You already know how to add matrices and scale them up. But multiplying two matrices isn't just crunching numbers straight across. Matrix multiplication is about chaining systems together. We multiply entries of the first matrix with the entries of the second matrix according to a specific pattern.

2. Intuition Building

Think of a Nuclear Thermal Rocket (NTR). You have a chain of physical dependencies.

  • 🔵 Matrix \( B \) transforms the control rod drum positions into the reactor core's thermal power output.
  • 🔵 Matrix \( A \) transforms that thermal power output into the propellant's exhaust velocity and total thrust.

If you want to know directly how a control rod rotation affects your thrust, you multiply the matrices: \( AB \). The output of the first system (Matrix B) must perfectly fit the input of the second system (Matrix A). If the reactor produces 3 distinct temperature zones (columns of B), the thrust chamber dynamics must be built to accept exactly 3 temperature inputs (rows of A). If the geometry doesn't align, the system physically crashes.

3. Formal Definition

To multiply Matrix \( A \) and Matrix \( B \), they must pass a strict dimensional check.

  • 🔵 Inner Dimensions Must Match: Finding the product of two matrices is only possible when the inner dimensions are the same, meaning that the number of columns of the first matrix is equal to the number of rows of the second matrix.
  • 🔵 The Product Matrix: If \( A \) is an \( m \times r \) matrix and \( B \) is an \( r \times n \) matrix, then the product matrix \( AB \) is an \( m \times n \) matrix.
  • 🔵 The Dot Product Rule: To obtain the entries in row \( i \) of \( AB \), we multiply the entries in row \( i \) of \( A \) by column \( j \) in \( B \) and add.
  • 🔵 Commutativity: Matrix multiplication is not commutative. \( AB \neq BA \). Reversing the engine components doesn't give you the same physics!

Example 9.34: Multiplying Two Matrices

Given \( A \) and \( B \):
a. Find \( AB \).
b. Find \( BA \).

\[ A = \begin{bmatrix} -1 & 2 & 3 \\ 4 & 0 & 5 \end{bmatrix} \text{ and } B = \begin{bmatrix} 5 & -1 \\ -4 & 0 \\ 2 & 3 \end{bmatrix} \]

Solution (a)

As the dimensions of \( A \) are \( 2 \times 3 \) and the dimensions of \( B \) are \( 3 \times 2 \), these matrices can be multiplied together because the number of columns in \( A \) matches the number of rows in \( B \). The resulting product will be a \( 2 \times 2 \) matrix, the number of rows in \( A \) by the number of columns in \( B \).

\[ AB = \begin{bmatrix} -1 & 2 & 3 \\ 4 & 0 & 5 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 5 & -1 \\ -4 & 0 \\ 2 & 3 \end{bmatrix} \] \[ = \begin{bmatrix} -1(5) + 2(-4) + 3(2) & -1(-1) + 2(0) + 3(3) \\ 4(5) + 0(-4) + 5(2) & 4(-1) + 0(0) + 5(3) \end{bmatrix} \] \[ = \begin{bmatrix} -7 & 10 \\ 30 & 11 \end{bmatrix} \]

Does this make sense? Row runs into Column. Multiply pairs, add them up.

Solution (b)

The dimensions of \( B \) are \( 3 \times 2 \) and the dimensions of \( A \) are \( 2 \times 3 \). The inner dimensions match so the product is defined and will be a \( 3 \times 3 \) matrix.

\[ BA = \begin{bmatrix} 5 & -1 \\ -4 & 0 \\ 2 & 3 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} -1 & 2 & 3 \\ 4 & 0 & 5 \end{bmatrix} \] \[ = \begin{bmatrix} 5(-1) + -1(4) & 5(2) + -1(0) & 5(3) + -1(5) \\ -4(-1) + 0(4) & -4(2) + 0(0) & -4(3) + 0(5) \\ 2(-1) + 3(4) & 2(2) + 3(0) & 2(3) + 3(5) \end{bmatrix} \] \[ = \begin{bmatrix} -9 & 10 & 10 \\ 4 & -8 & -12 \\ 10 & 4 & 21 \end{bmatrix} \]

Analysis: Notice that the products \( AB \) and \( BA \) are not equal. This illustrates the fact that matrix multiplication is not commutative.

5. Common Pitfalls
  • 🔵 Inner Dimension Mismatch: Trying to multiply a \( 3 \times 4 \) matrix by a \( 2 \times 3 \) matrix. It will crash. Always write out dimensions first: \( (3 \times \mathbf{4}) \times (\mathbf{2} \times 3) \). Since \( \mathbf{4 \neq 2} \), it's undefined.
  • 🔵 Assuming Commutativity: Assuming \( AB = BA \). In physics and engineering, order of operations matters. Matrix operations are highly dependent on the left-to-right setup.
