1. Concept Overview

A matrix is simply a rectangular array of numbers, organized into horizontal rows and vertical columns. When working with systems of data, matrices let us combine or track changes efficiently. Adding and subtracting matrices is just combining the corresponding cells in those grids.

2. Intuition Building

Let’s anchor this. Imagine the core of a Nuclear Thermal Rocket (NTR). We use a spatial grid to map the neutron flux distribution across the reactor core. Matrix \( A \) represents the baseline neutron flux in the fuel assemblies. Matrix \( B \) represents the localized change in flux during a control rod transient.

To find the total, updated flux distribution, you literally just lay the grids on top of each other and add the matching coordinates. But here’s the physical constraint: you can only add them if the grids map the exact same geometry. If Matrix \( A \) maps a \( 3 \times 3 \) grid and Matrix \( B \) maps a \( 2 \times 3 \) grid, they are physically incompatible. You cannot add or subtract them because some data points lack a corresponding partner.

3. Formal Definition
  • 🔵 Dimensional Requirement: Addition and subtraction of matrices is only possible when the matrices have the same dimensions.
  • 🔵 Matrix Addition: Given matrices \( A \) and \( B \) of like dimensions, \( A + B = C \) such that \( a_{ij} + b_{ij} = c_{ij} \).
  • 🔵 Matrix Subtraction: Given matrices \( A \) and \( B \) of like dimensions, \( A - B = D \) such that \( a_{ij} - b_{ij} = d_{ij} \).
  • 🔵 Notation: \( i \) defines the row, and \( j \) defines the column. For example, \( a_{23} \) is the entry in row 2, column 3.
4. Worked Examples

Let's narrate this step-by-step using verbatim examples from your text.

Example 9.28 Adding Matrix A and Matrix B

Find the sum of \( A \) and \( B \).

\[ A = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 1 \\ 3 & 2 \end{bmatrix} \text{ and } B = \begin{bmatrix} 5 & 9 \\ 0 & 7 \end{bmatrix} \]

Solution: Add corresponding entries. Add the entry in row 1, column 1, \( a_{11} \), of matrix \( A \) to the entry in row 1, column 1, \( b_{11} \), of \( B \). Continue the pattern until all entries have been added.

\[ A + B = \begin{bmatrix} 4+5 & 1+9 \\ 3+0 & 2+7 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 9 & 10 \\ 3 & 9 \end{bmatrix} \]

Does this make sense? Just stacking the grids.

Example 9.29 Finding the Difference of Two Matrices

Find the difference of \( A \) and \( B \).

\[ A = \begin{bmatrix} -2 & 3 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \text{ and } B = \begin{bmatrix} 8 & 1 \\ 5 & 4 \end{bmatrix} \]

Solution: We subtract the corresponding entries of each matrix.

\[ A - B = \begin{bmatrix} -2-8 & 3-1 \\ 0-5 & 1-4 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -10 & 2 \\ -5 & -3 \end{bmatrix} \]

Are you tracking the negative signs here?

5. Common Pitfalls
  • 🔵 Dimensional Mismatch: Trying to add a \( 2 \times 3 \) matrix to a \( 3 \times 3 \) matrix. It will flat out fail because entries in one matrix won't have a corresponding entry in the other. Don't force geometry that doesn't fit!
  • 🔵 Dropping the Negative: When doing subtraction (\( A - B \)), failing to distribute the minus sign to negative entries in \( B \). Watch your signs like you watch your reactor pressure.
