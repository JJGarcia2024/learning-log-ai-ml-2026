### PRIMARY TRAINING MODALITY: SWIMMING

**Design Principle — v2.0:**
Swimming is now the PRIMARY cross-training and cardiovascular conditioning modality for Wordsmith's
off-ice and off-season training. This is evidence-based and non-negotiable.

Swimming eliminates ground reaction forces entirely, providing a full cardiovascular stimulus with
no axial loading on the healing or previously fractured bone. Given Wordsmith's fibula fracture
history, running and other high-impact cardio carry meaningful recurrence risk that pool training
avoids entirely. [1] Figure skaters are chronically prone to fibula, tibia, and metatarsal stress
fractures from repeated microtrauma without sufficient rest. [2] Swimming distributes load across
all major muscle groups and gives the skating-specific muscles — peroneals, calves, hip flexors,
ankle stabilizers — recovery time without losing cardiovascular fitness.

Figure skaters also chronically under-develop peroneal muscles by over-relying on the rigid boot
for ankle support, making them acutely vulnerable to lateral ankle injuries and fibula stress
fractures. [3] Swim kick mechanics actively develop peroneal and dorsiflexor strength in a low-risk
environment. Freestyle and backstroke kicking drills specifically challenge ankle plantar flexion
and dorsiflexion range of motion — the exact mobility deficits that follow fibula fractures.
Athletes who returned to swimming after fibula fractures reported being able to begin aquatic rehab
well before land-based training was possible, using water aerobics to rebuild balance and muscle
memory. [4]

Water also provides constant proprioceptive feedback and buoyancy support that reduces injury risk
for dyspraxic athletes who may stumble or disorient on hard surfaces.

### Swimming Programme — Weekly Template

Distances and intensities must be adjusted in consultation with Wordsmith's physiotherapist following
clearance from their orthopedic surgeon regarding fibula fracture status. All swim sessions are 45
to 60 minutes total pool time including warm-up and cool-down.

🔵 **Monday — Technique and Endurance (Low Intensity):**
Warm-up: 4 x 25m easy freestyle, focus on long stroke and bilateral breathing. Main set: 6 x 50m
freestyle at conversational pace (RPE 3-4 out of 10). Kick set: 4 x 25m kickboard freestyle kick
only — this directly rehabs ankle dorsiflexion and peroneal engagement. Cool-down: 200m easy
backstroke or sidestroke. Total: approximately 600 to 800m.

🔵 **Wednesday — Aerobic Power (Moderate Intensity):**
Warm-up: 200m easy mixed strokes. Main set: 4 x 100m freestyle with 30 seconds rest, targeting
sustained pace (RPE 6). Backstroke set: 4 x 50m backstroke — develops shoulder girdle and upper
back postural strength critical for skating carriage. Cool-down: 100m easy. Total: approximately
900 to 1000m.

🔵 **Saturday — Active Recovery Swim or Aqua Jogging:**
On weeks with on-ice training, replace hard swim with 20 to 30 minutes of aqua jogging in the deep
end using a flotation belt. This maintains cardiovascular work without any eccentric impact — ideal
post-skating-session recovery. If no on-ice session this week, perform the Monday template at easy pace.

**Key swim drills for figure skater benefit:**

🔵 **Kickboard drills** — isolate ankle plantar flexion and dorsiflexion under water resistance.
Directly addresses the mobility deficits that follow fibula fractures and rigid boot use.

🔵 **Backstroke** — develops posterior shoulder, latissimus dorsi, and upper back — the muscle
groups responsible for skating carriage, arm lines, and spin axis stability.

🔵 **Pull buoy drills** — isolate upper body and core without any leg kick, giving the ankle and
peroneal complex full rest on high-training-volume weeks.

🔵 **Aqua jogging** — simulates running gait with zero ground reaction force. Maintains cardiovascular
conditioning and hip flexor endurance essential for program run-throughs. [5]

**Progression milestones:**
At 12 weeks: target continuous 800m freestyle with good technique. At 24 weeks: 1500m continuous,
incorporating mixed strokes. The primary outcome is zero skating-related overuse injury recurrence
and sustained cardiovascular fitness year-round — not swimming performance metrics.

### FIBULA FRACTURE PREVENTION AND RETURN-TO-SKATE PROTOCOL

This section must be referenced for ALL skating training recommendations. Wordsmith has a fibula
fracture history. This is a permanent chronic injury risk factor. ALL skating programming must be
designed around this fact.

Fibula stress fractures are among the most frequent chronic injuries in recreational and competitive
skaters, caused by repeated microtrauma from jump takeoffs and landings, rigid boot use, and
insufficient rest periods between training blocks. [2] The primary prevention strategy is to build
peroneal and intrinsic ankle strength so the ankle is not entirely dependent on the boot. Restoring
adequate ankle dorsiflexion is paramount in rehabilitation — stretching should be relatively
pain-free and can be performed several times daily. [6]

**Phase 1 — Aquatic Rehabilitation (Weeks 0-8, physician-cleared):**
🔵 Water walking and aqua jogging in pool depth at hip to chest level.
🔵 Gentle ankle circles and plantar flexion/dorsiflexion in water — no kicking intensity.
🔵 Freestyle swimming with pull buoy (no leg kick) — maintains upper body conditioning.
🔵 Progressive introduction of gentle flutter kick only when physician confirms bone callus formation. [1][7]

**Phase 2 — Land Rehab and Off-Ice Conditioning (Weeks 6-12, physiotherapy-guided):**
🔵 Calf raises (bilateral progressing to single-leg) for gastrocnemius and soleus loading.
🔵 Resistance band peroneal eversion drills — the single most critical exercise for fibula protection.
🔵 Balance board single-leg standing: 30 seconds progressing to 60 seconds with eyes closed.
🔵 Ankle dorsiflexion stretching daily — restores range of motion restricted by boot use and cast.
🔵 No plyometrics or jump training until cleared by orthopedic surgeon. [6][3]

**Phase 3 — Return to Ice (Weeks 10-16, sport-physiotherapy-guided):**
🔵 Stroking only for first two to three on-ice sessions. No jumping.
🔵 Crossovers and backward skating introduced at Week 12 if pain-free.
🔵 Jump simulation on off-ice harness before attempting on-ice jumps.
🔵 Single-rotation jumps (waltz jump, toe loop) only, limited to 10 attempts per session initially.
🔵 Limit repetitive jump attempts per individual jump — the risk of fibula re-fracture spikes
  sharply when repeatedly drilling a single new jump. [2]
🔵 Ice sessions capped at 30 minutes for first two weeks back. [8]

**Ongoing prevention non-negotiables (permanent):**
🔵 Peroneal eversion band work: 3 sets of 15 reps, minimum 3x per week, forever.
🔵 Single-leg balance on wobble board: 5 minutes daily off-ice.
🔵 Jump repetition limit: maximum 8 to 10 repetitions of any single jump per session.
🔵 Boot fit check every 6 months. Worn-out boots increase fibula stress loading.
🔵 Swimming maintained year-round as the primary cardio modality. Not optional.
